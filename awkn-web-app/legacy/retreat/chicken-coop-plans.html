1. Design Overview & Layout

This coop has two sections under one continuous gable roof:

  • Enclosed Coop (left end) — 10' wide × 8' deep. Solid wood walls, 4 nesting boxes on the back wall, a diagonal roosting perch, and a clean-out door on the back.
  • Covered Run (right side) — 10' wide × 12' long. Open-air with ½" hardware cloth on all sides and the bottom for predator protection. Main entry door on the right-side wall.

4. Phase 1 — Foundation & Floor Frame

We're building on concrete pier blocks (deck blocks) — no concrete pouring needed. This keeps the coop off the ground (prevents rot and lets air circulate) and makes it level on uneven ground.

Figure 4 — Foundation & Floor Joist Layout
1

Lay Out & Level the Site

Clear a 12' × 22' area (extra 1' on each side). Use stakes and string to mark the 10' × 20' rectangle. Check that corners are square by measuring diagonals — they should be equal (22' 4⅜" for a 10×20).

2

Place Pier Blocks

Set 8 concrete pier blocks at the locations shown in Figure 4. Use a long level and a straight board to make sure all blocks are level with each other. Dig out or add gravel as needed.

  • 3 blocks along each long side (front and back) at 0', 8', and 20'
  • 2 blocks at the center beam at 0' and 8' (coop section only)
3

Build the Sill Frame

Using pressure-treated 2×6 lumber:

  • Cut two 20' sills for the long sides
  • Cut three 10' sills for the short sides (both ends + center divider at the 8' mark)
  • Set them on the pier blocks and fasten with 3" deck screws at corners
  • Check for square again with diagonal measurements
4

Install Floor Joists (Coop Section Only)

Inside the coop section (the 10' × 8' rectangle on the left):

  • Install 2×6 joists at 16" on-center, running the 10' direction
  • Use joist hangers at each end for a solid connection
  • You'll need 7 joists total (including at each end)
5

Install Coop Floor Decking

Lay ¾" exterior plywood over the joists. Screw down every 8" along each joist with 2" screws. Stagger the seams. This gives you a solid, cleanable floor.

The run section has NO floor — chickens scratch on the ground. But you'll bury hardware cloth 12" out as an apron around the run perimeter to stop predators from digging under (see Phase 4).

5. Phase 2 — Wall Framing

Figure 5 — Wall Framing Detail

Coop Walls (Enclosed Section)

1

Frame the Coop Walls on the Ground

Build each wall flat on the ground, then raise it into position. For each wall:

  1. Cut a bottom plate and top plate to length
  2. Cut studs at 69" (5' 9") — this gives 6' total with plates
  3. Space studs 16" on-center
  4. Screw studs to plates with two 3" screws at each end

Cut List — Coop Walls

  • Back wall (10'): 2× plates at 10', 8× studs at 69", header & cripples for clean-out opening (48"W × 36"H) and nesting box access (54"W × 18"H)
  • Front wall (10'): 2× plates at 10', 8× studs at 69", headers for 2 windows (24"W × 18"H each)
  • Left end wall (8'): 2× plates at 8', 5× studs at 69"
  • Divider wall (10'): 2× plates at 10', 7× studs at 69", pop door opening (12"W × 14"H)
2

Raise & Secure Coop Walls

Raise each wall and screw the bottom plate to the floor frame. Use a level to make sure each wall is plumb (perfectly vertical). Temporarily brace with diagonal 2×4s. Screw adjoining walls together at corners with 3" screws every 12".

3

Add Double Top Plate

Add a second top plate overlapping the corners for strength. This locks all the walls together.

Run Walls (Open Mesh Section)

4

Set Run Posts

The run uses 4×4 posts at each corner and every 4' along the long sides:

  • Front side (12'): 4 posts at 0', 4', 8', 12'
  • Back side: same layout
  • Right end wall: 3 posts at 0', 5', 10'

Cut all posts at 6'. Bolt them to the sill frame with carriage bolts.

5

Add Top & Mid Rails

Run 2×4 horizontal rails across the top of posts and at 3' height (mid-rail). These give you something to staple hardware cloth to and add rigidity.

6

Frame the Main Door Opening

On the right end wall, frame a 30" wide × 72" tall door opening between two posts. Add a header (doubled 2×4) across the top.

6. Phase 3 — Roof Framing & Shingles

Figure 6 — Roof Cross-Section (6:12 pitch)
1

Install the Ridge Board

The ridge board is a single 2×6 × 20' running the full length of the structure at the peak. You'll need a helper for this step.

  • The ridge sits at 8'6" from the floor (6' walls + 2'6" rise)
  • Temporarily support it with a 2×4 post at each end
2

Cut & Install Rafters

Using 2×4s, cut 22 rafters (11 pairs) spaced at 24" on-center:

  • Rafter length: ~6'8" each (5' run ÷ cos(26.5°) + 12" overhang)
  • Cut a bird's mouth notch where each rafter sits on the top plate — this is a 90° notch that hooks over the wall
  • Cut a plumb cut at the ridge end to sit flush against the ridge board
  • Nail each rafter to the ridge board and toenail to the top plate
  • Add hurricane ties at the plate connection for wind resistance
Make ONE rafter, test-fit it, then use it as a template to mark all the others. This ensures every rafter is identical.
3

Add Collar Ties

Install 2×4 collar ties horizontally between every other rafter pair, about ⅓ down from the ridge. These prevent the roof from spreading outward.

4

Install Roof Sheathing

Lay ½" CDX plywood over the rafters, starting from the bottom edge. Stagger the seams (like brickwork). Nail every 6" along rafters with 8d nails.

5

Install Drip Edge

Nail aluminum drip edge along all eaves (bottom edges) FIRST, then along the rakes (side edges) ON TOP of the felt paper.

6

Roll Out Roofing Felt

Starting at the bottom, roll 15 lb felt paper horizontally. Overlap each row by 4". Staple every 8-12". The felt goes OVER the eave drip edge but UNDER the rake drip edge.

7

Install Shingles

  1. Starter strip: Lay a row of shingles upside-down (or use starter strip shingles) along the eave, overhanging ½"
  2. First course: Lay shingles right-side-up directly on top of the starter strip
  3. Subsequent courses: Offset each row by half a tab (6") and overlap by 5". Use 4 nails per shingle, placed just below the adhesive strip
  4. Ridge caps: Cut individual tabs from a 3-tab shingle (or use ridge cap shingles). Fold over the ridge and nail on each side. Overlap each cap by 5"
Always work from the bottom of the roof UP, so upper shingles overlap lower ones and water sheds properly. Never nail through the exposed portion of a shingle.

7. Phase 4 — Siding & Wire Mesh

Coop Siding

1

Install Coop Siding

For the rustic barn look (matching the photos):

  • Screw ½" plywood sheathing to the coop wall studs first (for strength and weatherproofing)
  • Then nail 1×6 barn-style boards vertically or horizontally over the plywood for the rustic appearance
  • Cut out openings for windows and doors with a jigsaw
  • Alternatively, use T1-11 siding (looks like board & batten) for a simpler approach
2

Frame & Screen Windows

The coop front wall has two windows (24" × 18" each). Frame each opening with 1×4 trim. Cover with ½" hardware cloth on the inside. Optionally add hinged plywood shutters on the outside for winter.

Run — Wire Mesh (Predator Proofing)

Figure 7 — Predator-Proof Wire Mesh Installation
3

Install Hardware Cloth on Run Walls

Unroll ½" hardware cloth and attach to the INSIDE of the run posts (so predators push it against the wood, not away from it).

  • Use heavy-duty staples every 3-4 inches along all frame members
  • Add fender washers + screws every 12" for extra security (raccoons can pull staples)
  • Overlap mesh sections by at least 4" and wire them together
  • Cover ALL openings — walls, gable ends above the run
4

Install Anti-Dig Apron

This is crucial for predator proofing:

  • Attach a 12"-wide strip of hardware cloth to the bottom of each run wall
  • Bend it outward at 90° so it lays flat on the ground
  • Bury it 2" under soil or cover with pavers/gravel
  • When a predator tries to dig at the wall base, it hits the buried mesh and gives up
5

Fill Gable Ends

The triangular gable ends above the walls on both the coop and run sections need to be covered:

  • Coop gables: plywood siding to match
  • Run gables: hardware cloth

8. Phase 5 — Interior: Nesting Boxes, Perch & Ramp

Figure 8 — Coop Interior Layout (cutaway, looking from front)

Nesting Boxes

1

Build the Nesting Box Unit

Build TWO units of 2 boxes each (4 boxes total). Each box is 12" wide × 12" deep × 12" tall.

Cut List — Nesting Boxes (from ½" plywood)

Per unit of 2 boxes:

  • Bottom: 24" × 12" (1 piece)
  • Back: 24" × 12" (1 piece)
  • Sides: 12" × 12" (2 pieces — left and right ends)
  • Center divider: 12" × 12" (1 piece)
  • Front lip: 24" × 4" (1 piece — keeps bedding in)
  • Top/lid: 26" × 14" (1 piece — overhangs for weather seal, hinged at back)

× 2 units = 4 nesting boxes total

2

Assemble & Mount Nesting Boxes

  • Glue and screw each unit together with 1⅝" screws
  • Mount the units on the BACK wall of the coop, centered, with the bottom of the boxes 24" off the floor
  • Screw through the back of each unit into the wall studs
  • Hinge the top lids so they open OUTWARD from the back — this gives you easy egg collection and cleaning from outside through the nesting box access opening
  • Add a 45° angled board on top of the box units to prevent chickens from roosting (and pooping) on top
Place nesting boxes in the darkest part of the coop. Hens prefer dim, private spaces for laying.

Diagonal Roosting Perch

3

Install the Diagonal Perch

This is the signature feature — a single 2×4 running diagonally across the coop, giving each hen 10" of perch space.

  • High end: Mount at 4' off the floor on the left wall (opposite the pop door), using heavy L-brackets bolted to a wall stud
  • Low end: Mount at 18" off the floor on the right wall (near the divider/pop door), also with L-brackets
  • Orient the 2×4 with the wide (4") side UP — chickens grip with their toes and the wide surface prevents frostbite
  • Round the top edges with sandpaper so it's comfortable for their feet
  • The diagonal length is approximately 9' — perfect for 10 chickens at ~10" each
The perch must NOT be directly over the nesting boxes, or the chickens will poop in the boxes. Position it so droppings fall on the open floor area (easy to clean).
4

Optional: Droppings Board

Mount a removable sheet of plywood or a shallow tray under the perch (about 6-8" below it) to catch droppings. Slide it out from the clean-out door for easy disposal. This keeps the floor cleaner and makes daily maintenance faster.

Chicken Ramp

5

Build & Install the Ramp

  • Cut a 1×10 board to about 4-5' long (depending on the height difference between coop floor and ground)
  • Nail 1×2 strips across it every 4" as treads (so chickens can grip)
  • Lean it from the pop door opening down to the run ground at about a 35-40° angle
  • Attach the top end to the pop door frame with a hinge or L-bracket (so you can flip it up when needed)

9. Phase 6 — Doors & Hardware

Figure 9 — All Four Doors

Door A — Main Entry Door (Right Side of Run)

1

Build the Main Door

  • Frame a door from 2×4s: 30" wide × 72" tall
  • Add a diagonal brace (Z-brace pattern) for rigidity
  • Cover with hardware cloth on the inside (to match the run)
  • Optionally add siding on the lower half for a Dutch door look
  • Hang with 2 heavy-duty T-hinges on the right side (swings out)
  • Add barrel bolt latch + carabiner clip for predator-proof locking

Door B — Coop Clean-Out Door (Back Wall, Bottom)

2

Build the Clean-Out Door

  • This is a wide door (48" × 36") on the back wall of the coop
  • Build from plywood with a 2×4 frame around the edge
  • Hinge at the BOTTOM so it drops down — this lets you shovel bedding straight out
  • Add 2 barrel bolt latches (one at each top corner)
  • Caulk the edges to prevent drafts
The clean-out door hinged at the bottom drops down to become a ramp — bedding slides right out into a wheelbarrow parked behind the coop!

Door C — Nesting Box Access (Back Wall, Top)

3

Build Nesting Box Access Lid

  • This is a hinged lid (54" × 18") on the back wall above the nesting boxes
  • Hinge at the TOP so it swings up and stays propped open
  • Add a chain or prop stick to hold it open while you collect eggs
  • Add a barrel bolt latch at the bottom

Door D — Chicken Pop Door (Divider Wall)

4

Install the Pop Door

  • Cut a 12" × 14" opening in the divider wall, about 6" above the coop floor
  • Build a simple sliding door from ½" plywood that slides in a track (two 1×2 strips on each side)
  • Optional: run a pull rope through a pulley to the outside so you can open/close the pop door without entering the coop
  • Install the chicken ramp on the run side, sloping down to ground level

10. Phase 7 — Finishing Touches

1

Seal & Stain All Wood

  • Apply exterior wood stain or sealant to ALL exposed wood
  • Pay special attention to end grain (where cuts were made) — these absorb water fast
  • Do NOT stain the interior floor — just use pine shavings on bare wood so you can scrape it clean
  • Let everything dry 24-48 hours before adding chickens
2

Add Ventilation

  • Cut two 6" × 24" vents high on the coop walls (one on each gable end, near the peak)
  • Cover with hardware cloth
  • These provide cross-ventilation without creating drafts at roost level
  • Good ventilation prevents moisture buildup and ammonia — critical for chicken health
3

Install Bedding

  • Coop floor: 4-6" of pine shavings (NOT cedar — toxic to chickens)
  • Nesting boxes: straw or pine shavings, 3-4" deep
  • Run: leave as natural ground, or add sand/gravel for drainage
4

Safety Check Before Adding Chickens

  • Walk around the entire structure and check for ANY gaps larger than ½" — seal them
  • Tug on all hardware cloth to make sure it's secure
  • Test every door latch — can you open it with one hand while wearing gloves? If so, a raccoon can open it. Add carabiner clips.
  • Check for exposed screws or sharp wire ends that could injure chickens — cover or grind down
  • Make sure the perch is solid and doesn't wobble
  • Verify the pop door slides freely
Raccoons are incredibly dexterous — they can open simple latches, turn knobs, and lift hooks. ALWAYS use a two-step lock (barrel bolt + carabiner clip) on every door.
